Vaginal Infections

InfectionCauseSigns / SymptomsDiagnosisTreatment
Bacterial Vaginosis Decreased normal vaginal flora (frequent sex, douching) Thin, watery, whitish-gray discharge with fishy odor Slide prep; vaginal pH > 4.5 Metronidazole (Flagyl), clindamycin vaginal cream
Candidiasis (yeast) Antibiotics, oral contraceptives, immunosuppression, diabetes Thick, curd-like white discharge; severe itching, perineal/vulvar rash Spores on microscopy Fluconazole (Diflucan) or nystatin

Infertility

Essential Female Fertility Factors

  • Favorable cervical mucus
  • Patent, normally motile fallopian tubes
  • Ovaries producing/releasing normal eggs
  • No obstruction between ovary and uterus
  • Favorable endometrium
  • Adequate reproductive hormones

Essential Male Fertility Factors

  • Normal sperm quality, quantity, motility
  • Unobstructed genital tract
  • Normal genital tract secretions
  • Sperm able to reach the cervix

Primary infertility: no conception after 12 months of unprotected sex. Secondary infertility: unable to conceive/sustain pregnancy after a prior successful pregnancy. Tips to improve fertility: avoid douching/artificial lubricants, retain sperm 20–30 min post-intercourse, intercourse every other day during the fertile window, manage stress, address nutrition (both under- and overweight can affect fertility).

Infertility Workup

MethodWhat It Assesses
Basal body temperature (BBT)Body temperature rises/spikes when progesterone increases after ovulation; charting it helps pinpoint when ovulation occurs
Cervical mucus monitoringNear ovulation the discharge becomes thinner, more abundant, and more elastic — a sign of potential ovulation
Hormone assessmentChecks FSH and LH levels; the day of the LH surge is typically the most fertile day
Endometrial biopsyChecks the effect of progesterone on the endometrium after ovulation
Transvaginal ultrasoundEvaluates anatomy (two ovaries, patent tubes), blood flow, and egg maturity
Male workupEvaluates for ductal obstruction and abnormal sperm or sperm-production problems

Psychosocial effects of infertility: strain on the relationship, guilt/frustration, anger/shame, loss of control, feelings of incompetence, social stigma, strained relationship with the provider.

Genetics

Karyotype: pictorial view of an individual's chromosomes. Phenotype: the observable expression of a trait (hair/eye color, etc.). Humans have 22 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair of sex chromosomes.

Autosomal Dominant vs. Recessive Inheritance

Autosomal Dominant

  • Affected individual has an affected parent
  • 50% chance of passing the defect to each child
  • Only one abnormal allele needed
  • Examples: Huntington's disease, dwarfism, polydactyly

Prenatal Genetic Testing

TestWhat It Evaluates
UltrasoundVisualizes the fetus directly for structural abnormalities
AmniocentesisGenetic material from amniotic fluid — chromosomal disorders/syndromes; used for advanced maternal age, prior affected child, abnormal screen
Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)Genetic/chromosomal material from chorionic villi
Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)Maternal blood test — high = neural tube defects (spina bifida); low = increased Down syndrome risk

Self-Test Flashcards

Click a card to flip it and check your answer.

What's the classic diagnostic finding for bacterial vaginosis?
Thin, gray, fishy-smelling discharge with a vaginal pH greater than 4.5
How is genital herpes (HSV-2) managed in pregnancy, and what happens if there's an active lesion at delivery?
Start acyclovir at 36 weeks to suppress an outbreak (it suppresses, it doesn't cure). An active lesion at delivery means a C-section — vaginal exposure can cause a fatal systemic infection in the newborn.
RPR and VDRL are blood tests for which infection?
Syphilis (RPR is the preferred test)
What lab findings are associated with toxic shock syndrome?
Increased BUN, increased liver enzymes (AST/ALT), increased bilirubin, and low platelets
What's the difference between primary and secondary infertility?
Primary = no conception after 12 months of unprotected sex, ever. Secondary = unable to conceive/sustain a pregnancy after a prior successful one.
Which infertility medication inhibits prolactin to help induce ovulation?
Bromocriptine (Parlodel)
What's the difference between IVF and GIFT?
IVF fertilizes the egg in a lab before placing the embryo in the uterus. GIFT places egg and sperm together in the fallopian tube so fertilization happens naturally in the body.
What's the genetic makeup of Turner syndrome vs. Klinefelter syndrome?
Turner = monosomy X (a single, inactive X chromosome), female. Klinefelter = XXY (an extra X chromosome), male.
Two carrier parents (Bb × Bb) have a child. Work the Punnett square — what are the odds for an autosomal recessive condition?
25% affected (bb), 50% unaffected carriers (Bb), 25% completely unaffected (BB) — for each pregnancy
How many abnormal alleles are needed to be affected by an autosomal dominant disorder? By an autosomal recessive one?
Dominant needs just one (so there's no silent carrier). Recessive needs two — one copy makes you an unaffected carrier.
A high AFP level suggests what risk? A low AFP?
High = open neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly). Low = increased risk of Down syndrome or trisomy 18.
How does chorionic villus sampling differ from amniocentesis?
Both retrieve genetic/chromosomal material — CVS samples the chorionic villi (placenta), while amniocentesis samples the amniotic fluid
