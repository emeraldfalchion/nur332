Antepartum Complications

Diabetes in Pregnancy

An endocrine disorder of carbohydrate metabolism from inadequate insulin production or use. Classic signs: polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia, weight loss.

TypeDescription
Type 1Lack of insulin production
Type 2Insufficient insulin production
GestationalInsufficient insulin production due to pregnancy
Insulin needs across pregnancy: In the first trimester the need decreases (pregnancy hormones enhance insulin production/response). From late first trimester onward the need increases — pregnancy hormones act as insulin antagonists, and the need may double or quadruple by term.

Risks of Diabetes in Pregnancy

Maternal risks

  • Polyhydramnios (increased fetal urine)
  • Preeclampsia / eclampsia
  • Ketoacidosis (DKA) — a major cause of stillbirth
  • Dystocia (difficult labor from a large baby)
  • Infections — UTIs and yeast (extra glucose feeds bacteria)
  • Retinopathy; perinatal mortality

Fetal / neonatal risks

  • Congenital anomalies (high glucose during organ formation)
  • Macrosomia (large baby) and, conversely, IUGR from poor placental perfusion
  • Respiratory distress syndrome (fetal insulin inhibits surfactant enzymes)
  • Polycythemia (extra RBCs)
  • Neonatal hypoglycemia after delivery
  • Hyperbilirubinemia (immature liver + polycythemia breakdown)

Keeping glucose in range helps prevent fetal macrosomia. Screening is usually at 24–28 weeks for low-risk patients, earlier for high-risk (obesity, family history, prior GDM, hypertension, glucose in the urine).

Management: diet therapy & exercise, glucose monitoring, insulin; dietitian referral; frequent ultrasounds, daily kick counts, NSTs. Deliver ≥39 weeks unless a complication. Assess glucose often (even hourly) in labor. Insulin needs drop sharply after delivery; breastfeeding helps regulate. Retest at 6 weeks postpartum — still elevated means type 2 diabetes.

Recognizing Substance Abuse

Prevention is the ideal nursing goal, so assessment and early identification matter. (The specific teratogenic substances are covered under Antepartum Care II.)

Possible signs

  • Vague or unusual complaints
  • History of abuse, depression, STIs, or prior drug use
  • Poor nutritional status; track marks
  • Mood swings, hallucinations
  • Frequent accidents and falls
  • Cirrhosis or hepatitis

Nursing approach

  • Teach the effects of substances on the mother and baby
  • Establish a trusting, non-judgmental relationship

HIV / AIDS in Pregnancy

Transmission & testing

  • Antibodies detectable ~6–12 weeks (up to 6 months) — a woman can be infectious before she knows
  • Spread by sexual contact, contaminated blood, and perinatally/congenitally (vertical transmission)
  • Screen all pregnancies; retest at-risk women in the third trimester

Reducing transmission

  • ZDV antiretroviral therapy (ART) lowers transmission risk
  • Treatment reduces perinatal transmission to ~1–2%
  • Avoid invasive procedures; use universal precautions (gloves for body fluids)
  • Elective C-section if indicated, before membranes rupture
  • Bottle-feed where formula is available (breast milk can carry HIV)

Cardiac Disorders in Pregnancy

Pregnancy increases cardiac output, heart rate, and blood volume — a strain when cardiac reserve is already reduced.

DisorderDescription
Congenital heart diseasePresent from birth — e.g., a septal defect or valve dysfunction
Peripartum cardiomyopathyLeft-ventricular dysfunction from the last month of pregnancy up to ~5 months postpartum; edema, cough, chest pain, fatigue
Eisenmenger syndromeLeft-to-right shunting that leads to pulmonary hypertension
Mitral valve prolapseMitral valve prolapses into the left atrium during systole; systolic murmur from regurgitation
Signs of cardiac decompensation (assess at each visit; note if progressive): new cough, dyspnea, edema, murmur, palpitations, rales, and weight gain. Management: 8–10 hrs of rest, diet high in iron/protein and low in sodium, epidural to reduce cardiac workload, and side-lying/semi-Fowler's positioning.

Miscarriages generally occur in the first trimester; ~50% of early losses are due to a chromosomal abnormality. Assess bleeding, vital signs, and the need for Rh immune globulin (RhoGAM for Rh-negative moms). May need a D&C (first trimester) or D&E (second trimester). Recurrent miscarriage = 3+ consecutive or >2 losses → genetic testing, antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, and thyroid disease workup.

Rh Alloimmunization

An Rh-negative mother exposed to Rh-positive fetal blood forms antibodies that break down fetal red blood cells. It usually doesn't harm the first baby but affects future pregnancies.

If untreated: destruction of fetal RBCs → fetal anemia, hydrops fetalis (extensive swelling; heart failure in ~50%, may be lethal), erythroblastosis fetalis, and kernicterus (neurological brain damage from high bilirubin).

Self-Test Flashcards

Click a card to flip it and check your answer.

Why does insulin need decrease early in pregnancy but increase later?
Early on, pregnancy hormones enhance insulin production and tissue response; later, those hormones act as insulin antagonists, so the need rises — sometimes doubling or quadrupling by term.
Why do infants of diabetic mothers often have respiratory distress syndrome?
High fetal insulin inhibits the enzymes needed for surfactant production
How is gestational diabetes diagnosed on the 3-hour glucose tolerance test?
If any TWO of the four values are elevated, she's a gestational diabetic. (You don't need the exact cutoffs — every lab uses different parameters.)
What are the glucose goals for a patient managing gestational diabetes?
Fasting < 95, one hour < 140, two hours < 120
What inheritance pattern is sickle cell anemia, and what triggers sickling?
Autosomal recessive (needs two copies of the recessive allele); red cells sickle when oxygen is decreased.
Why are HIV-positive mothers in this country encouraged to bottle-feed?
Breast milk is a body fluid that can carry traces of HIV, and formula is accessible here as a safe alternative.
Name the signs of cardiac decompensation to watch for at each prenatal visit.
New cough, dyspnea, edema, murmur, palpitations, rales, and weight gain
How does a threatened miscarriage differ from an imminent one?
Threatened: cervix closed, placenta still attached, with some bleeding. Imminent: cervix dilated and placenta separated, with bleeding.
What classic symptoms suggest an ectopic pregnancy?
Sharp one-sided pain, syncope, and referred shoulder pain; hCG that doesn't double every 2–3 days and low progesterone
What are the hallmark findings of a molar pregnancy?
Grape-like clusters, dark brown bleeding, a uterus larger than dates, no fetal heart tones, high hCG, and low AFP
When is RhoGAM given during pregnancy and after birth for an Rh-negative mother?
At 28 weeks if the antibody screen is negative, and within 72 hours after birth if the baby is Rh-positive with a negative direct Coombs.
If a mother's direct Coombs test is positive at birth, should you give RhoGAM?
No — a positive direct Coombs means she's already sensitized; instead, monitor the infant for hemolytic disease.
