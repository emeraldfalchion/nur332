Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

Overview

Hypertension is the most common medical complication in pregnancy, and preeclampsia is the #2 cause of maternal morbidity and mortality worldwide. The earlier hypertension develops, the greater the risk of severe preeclampsia later. Obesity increases the risk; hypertension raises the risk of placental abruption, preterm delivery, and IUGR.

Correct BP technique: cuff width ~40% of the arm, with 80% of the arm circumference covered; patient at rest ≥5 min, sitting or semi-reclining, arm at heart level.

Pathophysiology

In preeclampsia, prostacyclin (a vasodilator) is decreased, allowing vasoconstriction and reduced renal perfusion. This decreases placental perfusion → fetal hypoxia. Cell damage activates the coagulation cascade and redistributes intravascular fluid, ending in decreased organ perfusion for the placenta and other maternal organs.

Prevention: a low dose of 81 mg aspirin started at 12–28 weeks for at-risk patients helps reduce IUGR, preterm birth, and the development of preeclampsia.

Maternal & Fetal Effects

Maternal effects

  • Epigastric pain (liver) and elevated liver enzymes from vasoconstriction
  • CNS: headache, blurred vision, scotoma (blind spots, flashing lights, black dots)
  • Low platelets, DIC, renal failure, HELLP
  • Proteinuria, facial & pulmonary edema, ascites, pleural effusions
  • Oliguria (poorly perfused kidneys)

Fetal effects

  • Placental abruption
  • Abnormal umbilical artery Doppler (abnormal placental blood flow)
  • Oligohydramnios
  • Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR)
  • Stillbirth

Risk factors: chronic hypertension, chronic renal disease, diabetes, Rh incompatibility, first pregnancy, family history of preeclampsia, age <20 or >40, multiple gestation, IVF, and new paternity.

Assessment & Monitoring

Labs: CBC, liver enzymes, chemistry panel, type & screen. A 24-hour urine for protein and creatinine clearance is more accurate than a quick dipstick.

The more irritated the CNS, the more likely a seizure. Rising reflexes (e.g., 1+/2+ → 3+) signal worsening. Check for clonus by dorsiflexing the foot — taps back against your hand are "beats of clonus"; document each foot separately. You don't want clonus.

Magnesium Sulfate

Given to prevent convulsions/seizures. Used for eclampsia, severe preeclampsia, and HELLP. Typical loading dose 4–6 g over ~20 min, then a maintenance drip (often 2 g/hr IV). Continue for at least 24 hours postpartum. If a convulsion occurs, give another 2 g bolus.

Expected side effects

  • Headache
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Feeling hot and flushed
  • Tired / sleepy
  • Some muscle weakness

Signs of toxicity

  • Decreased or absent reflexes
  • Decreased respiratory rate
  • Change in level of consciousness / slurred speech
  • Arm too heavy to lift, trouble concentrating

Therapeutic range is about 4–7. The antidote for magnesium toxicity is IV calcium gluconate.

Eclampsia Management

Eclampsia is an obstetrical emergency. You don't try to stop the seizure — you prevent recurrence and protect the patient. Priority is the airway: keep her on her side, note the time/body parts/duration, prevent aspiration and injury (padded side rails, nothing in the mouth, suction as needed), give a 6 g magnesium sulfate bolus, then assess the fetus and watch for placental abruption. Delivery may be induced or done by emergency C-section. Seizures can still occur in the first week postpartum.

Self-Test Flashcards

Click a card to flip it and check your answer.

What BP defines gestational hypertension, what's notably absent — and how many readings does it take to diagnose?
SBP ≥140 or DBP ≥90 after 20 weeks, with no proteinuria. It is never diagnosed on one reading — you need TWO readings at least 4 hours apart.
How do the BP thresholds differ between mild and severe preeclampsia?
Mild: SBP ≥140 or DBP ≥90. Severe: SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥110 (both on two occasions ≥4 hours apart).
Which vasodilator is decreased in preeclampsia, and what does that cause?
Prostacyclin — its decrease allows vasoconstriction and reduced renal and placental perfusion.
What medication and dose is used to prevent preeclampsia in at-risk patients, and when is it started?
Low-dose aspirin, 81 mg, started at 12–28 weeks
Why does preeclampsia cause epigastric pain and elevated liver enzymes?
Vasoconstriction reduces blood flow to the liver, causing pain in the liver area and elevated enzymes.
What is the purpose of giving magnesium sulfate in preeclampsia?
To prevent convulsions/seizures — NOT to lower the blood pressure
What is the therapeutic magnesium level, and the antidote for toxicity?
Therapeutic level is 4–7; the antidote is IV calcium gluconate.
How do you tell a magnesium side effect from magnesium toxicity?
Side effects (expected): flushing, feeling hot, sleepiness, mild weakness. Toxicity: absent reflexes, decreased respiratory rate, and decreased level of consciousness / slurred speech.
What is the minimum acceptable urine output on magnesium — and how would you calculate it from a bag holding 100 mL after 4 hours?
At least 30 mL/hr. 100 mL ÷ 4 hr = 25 mL/hr, which is too low — magnesium is excreted by the kidneys, so poor output raises the risk of toxicity and must be reported.
What is the priority during an eclamptic seizure?
Maintaining the airway — keep the patient on her side and protect her from injury; you prevent recurrence rather than trying to stop the seizure.
How do you check for clonus, and what does its presence indicate?
Dorsiflex the foot and feel for taps back against your hand ("beats"); clonus indicates an irritated CNS and higher seizure risk.
Name the three acute severe-hypertension medications and the contraindication for each.
IV labetalol — avoid in asthma ("labetalol, lungs"). IV hydralazine — avoid in tachycardia. Oral nifedipine — also avoid in tachycardia. Know the drug, route, and contraindication; you don't need the doses.
