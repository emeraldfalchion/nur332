Maternal Nutrition

Role of Nutrients

NutrientRole in pregnancy
CarbohydratesPrimary source of energy; fiber supports bowel function (ensure adequate dietary fiber)
ProteinsMaternal tissue growth (uterus, breasts), fetal growth, energy metabolism
FatsEnergy; more completely absorbed in pregnancy (fetal fat deposits ~2% mid-pregnancy, ~12% at term)
Calcium & phosphorusMineralization of fetal bones and teeth; acid–base buffering
IodineEssential part of thyroxine (thyroid hormone); severe deficiency → cretinism. Good food source: iodized table salt.
SodiumMetabolism and fluid-balance regulation; not normally restricted in pregnancy
ZincProtein metabolism; DNA/RNA synthesis; fetal growth and lactation
MagnesiumCellular metabolism and bone mineralization
IronOxygen-carrying capacity of blood; expansion of maternal blood volume; low iron is the usual cause of anemia in pregnancy. Good food source: red meat.

Calorie & Nutrient Needs

Need in pregnancyDetails
Extra caloriesAbout +300 kcal/day during the second and third trimesters
Increased needsCarbohydrates, protein, calcium, zinc, magnesium, iron, vitamin D, vitamin C, and the B vitamins (thiamine, riboflavin, niacin, B6, B12)
No real change neededFat intake, vitamin E, vitamin K, iodine, and sodium
Only required supplementIf diet is adequate, iron is the only supplement truly needed — but since most diets fall short, a daily prenatal vitamin is recommended
MEMORIZE: A woman should get at least 400 mcg of folic acid per day during pregnancy or when trying to conceive.

Weight is distributed across all of these — not just the baby.

Hydration

  • Fluid drives biochemical reactions, lubrication, transport, and temperature control
  • Recommend eight to ten 8-oz glasses of fluid a day
  • At least half should be water

Pica

  • Craving and persistent eating of non-nutritive substances
  • Examples: clay, soil, powdered laundry starch, soap, baking powder
  • Can lead to iron-deficiency anemia — ask about it during nutritional assessment
Obesity risks in pregnancy: spontaneous abortion, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, labor induction, higher C-section rate, fetal anomalies, and a predisposition for the child to become obese later.

Self-Test Flashcards

Click a card to flip it and check your answer.

How much folic acid should a woman get daily during pregnancy or when trying to conceive?
At least 400 mcg per day — the one nutrient value she said to memorize. Too little is linked to neural tube defects.
How many extra calories per day are needed, and when?
About 300 extra kcal/day during the second and third trimesters
If a woman's diet is adequate, which single supplement is still truly needed — and what does it do?
Iron — it supports the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood and the expanding maternal blood volume
What deficiency can result from a severe iodine deficiency in the mother?
Cretinism in the infant — which is why iodized table salt is recommended
Which vitamins are fat-soluble and stored in the liver?
Vitamins A, D, E, and K
What is the role of vitamin K, and where is it found?
Synthesis of prothrombin for blood clotting; found in green leafy vegetables
Why is protein especially important in pregnancy?
It supports maternal tissue growth (uterus and breasts), fetal growth, and energy metabolism.
For which nutrients is no real change needed during pregnancy?
Fat intake, vitamin E, vitamin K, iodine, and sodium
How much fluid is recommended daily, and how much should be water?
Eight to ten 8-oz glasses a day, with at least half of that as water
What is pica, and what nutritional problem can it cause?
Craving and persistent eating of non-nutritive substances (clay, soil, laundry starch, soap); it can lead to iron-deficiency anemia.
A vegetarian patient has iron-deficiency anemia. What's the key principle in planning her diet?
Tailor the plan to her dietary preferences (don't just list red meat) and plan it in cooperation with the patient.
Name three risks of obesity in pregnancy.
Any three of: spontaneous abortion, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, labor induction, higher C-section rate, fetal anomalies, or childhood obesity.
