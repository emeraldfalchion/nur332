Intrapartum Care I — Labor & Birth

The Five Ps

The PWhat It Means
PassageThe birth passageway — the maternal pelvis, the cervix's ability to efface and dilate, and the ability of the vaginal canal and outlet to distend
PassengerThe fetus — its head, attitude, lie, and presentation
PositionThe relationship of the fetal presenting part to the maternal pelvis — includes station and engagement
PowersPrimary (involuntary uterine contractions) and secondary (voluntary maternal pushing) forces
PsycheThe patient's psychological state — fear, anxiety, excitement, fatigue, and her support system

1. Passage — the Birth Passageway

The baby travels through the bony pelvis → lower uterine segment → cervix (must thin and stretch) → pelvic floor muscles (help the fetus rotate) → vagina → introitus (the vaginal opening). The diameters of the pelvic inlet, mid-pelvis, and outlet, plus the curve of the sacrum, together determine whether a vaginal birth is possible.

The Four Pelvis Shapes

ShapeFrequencyCharacteristics & Implications for Labor
Gynecoid~50% (most common)The classic female pelvis. Hips wider and broader; ischial spines less prominent, making a wider opening. Most favorable for vaginal birth.
Android~23%Male-type pelvis with a longer sacrum and a more narrow arch. More likely to lead to CPD (cephalopelvic disproportion).
Anthropoid~24%Similar to apes. Increased risk of the baby being in an occiput posterior position.
Platypelloid~3% (least common)A flat pelvis. The baby may take longer to get into the pelvis, and is more likely to be in a transverse lie (side to side).

Effacement & Dilation

TermDefinitionHow It's Measured
EffacementThe gradual thinning/shortening of the cervix — the shortening of the distance between the internal and external cervical os0–100%
DilationThe gradual opening of the cervix0–10 cm

2. Passenger — the Fetus

The Fetal Head, Sutures & Fontanelles

The fetal head is made of six bones: the frontal bone, two parietal bones, two temporal bones, and one occipital bone. They are connected by four membranous sutures, and the fontanelles are where the sutures intersect. Sutures and fontanelles let the skull be flexible — allowing molding for delivery and continued brain growth. (Molding can leave the head slightly cone-shaped minutes after birth; it improves within 24 hours.)

FontanelleShapeSizeCloses
AnteriorDiamondThe largestBy about 18 months after birth
PosteriorTriangleSmallerMuch earlier — about 6–8 weeks after birth

Fetal Attitude

Attitude is the position of the baby's parts in relation to one another. The normal attitude: rounded back, chin flexed to the chest, thighs flexed on the abdomen, legs flexed at the knees, arms crossed. The biparietal diameter (BPD) is the important indicator of fetal head size.

Fetal Lie

Lie is the relationship of the fetal long axis to the mother's long axis.

LieWhat It Produces
Longitudinal (vertical)Head down = cephalic; bottom down = breech
Transverse (horizontal)A shoulder presentation

Fetal Presentation

BrowCephalicThe brow presents first — very difficult to deliver vaginally

3. Position, Station & Engagement

Fetal Station

Station is the relationship of the presenting part to the mother's ischial spines — the smallest, most narrow part of the pelvis. The ischial spines are zero station. Above the spines = a negative number (−1 to −5). Below the spines = a positive number (+1 to +5, with +5 being born). Station measures the degree of descent through the birth canal.

Engagement occurs when the presenting part reaches zero station. Before that — when the head is not down in the pelvis — the baby is described as ballotable and floating, i.e. not engaged.

Fetal Position — The Three-Letter Notation

Position is the relationship of the presenting part to the mother's pelvis, written with three landmarks. Right and left always refer to the MOTHER's right and left.

LetterWhat It Tells YouOptions
1st — sideWhich side of the maternal pelvis the presenting part is closest toR (right) or L (left)
2nd — landmarkWhat the fetal presenting part isO = occiput (head) · M = mentum (chin) · S = sacrum (bottom) · A = acromion process (scapula/shoulder)
3rd — directionWhether that part points toward the mother's front or backA (anterior) · P (posterior) · T (transverse)
What you want is an ANTERIOR position — the baby's face toward the floor at delivery, not face up. ROA = the occiput is on the mother's right and toward her anterior side. ROP = the occiput is still on her right, but toward her back — so the baby would be born face up, which is harder to push out.

4. Powers

Primary Forces — Uterine Contractions (Involuntary)

The primary forces are the uterine muscular contractions, and they are what is responsible for cervical effacement and dilation. They are involuntary.

TermDefinition
IncrementThe phase as the contraction builds/increases
AcmeThe peak of the contraction
DecrementThe phase as the contraction loosens and goes back down
Resting periodThe time between contractions — where you check the resting tone
FrequencyThe beginning of one contraction to the start of the next — measured in minutes
DurationThe start of a contraction to the end of that same contraction — measured in seconds
IntensityHow strong the contraction is — described as mild, moderate, or strong

Secondary Forces — Maternal Pushing (Voluntary)

Bearing down & the Ferguson reflex

  • Secondary forces are the mother voluntarily bearing down, using her abdominal muscles to help push.
  • The Ferguson reflex is the maternal urge to bear down, caused by the release of endogenous oxytocin when stretch receptors in the vagina are activated as the baby moves down.
  • Be careful of too much Valsalva maneuver — although commonly used, it reduces blood flow to the mother and the baby.

Why upright positions help

  • Walking, sitting, kneeling, and squatting all use gravity, which makes contractions stronger.
  • Upright positions increase cardiac output, and blood flow to the placenta, uterus, and maternal kidneys.
  • Frequent position changes relieve fatigue, increase comfort, and increase circulation.

5. Psyche

What patients feel

  • Fear and anxiety — of the pain, of loss of control, of themselves or the baby being hurt.
  • Excitement, joy, and anticipation — they have waited a long time for this baby.
  • Exhaustion, from the work of labor or just the stress of pregnancy.

Factors linked to a positive birth experience

  • Trusting her nurse, and being empowered to make her own decisions.
  • Having attended childbirth classes.
  • Anything that increases her self-confidence and self-esteem.
  • Receiving clear information about what you are doing with her.
  • Determine her level of social support — is there a partner or father of the baby involved? Who is her support system?

When the Five Ps Go Wrong

The PThe Abnormality That Can Adversely Affect Labor
PassagewayA pelvis that is too small — makes vaginal delivery difficult
PassengerA malpresentation or an odd position — e.g. occiput posterior, which is simply harder to push out
PowersInadequate contractions, or a patient who is too tired to push effectively
PsycheIntense fear, anxiety, poor support systems, and exhaustion

True Labor vs. False Labor

The single biggest discriminator: cervical change happens with TRUE labor.

FeatureTrue LaborFalse Labor
CervixProgressive change in dilation and effacementGenerally no change in dilation or effacement
Contraction patternRegular; get closer and closer togetherIrregular; usually no change in intervals or intensity
PainThey start hurting more over timeStays about the same
Effect of rest / warm bath / massageDoes NOT make them less painful or less intenseRest, warmth, and massage ease the pain

First Stage

PhaseDilationLengthContractionsHow She Feels
Latent
(early labor)		 0–3 cm Usually the longest phase. Nullipara/primigravida: average ~8 hours. Multipara/multigravida: shorter. Every 10–30 min, ~30 sec long, mild to moderate intensity Able to cope with the discomfort; relieved labor has finally started; recognizes and expresses anxiety; excitement is high and she is eager to talk
Active 4–7 cm First-time moms: average ~4 hours. Previous births: ~2 hours or so. This is usually when patients ask for the epidural. Good support here = greater satisfaction and less anxiety.
Transition 8–10 cm First-time moms: may last ~3 hours. Multips: may be less than an hour. Very frequent — every 1½–2 min, 60–90 sec long, very strong to palpation Withdraws into herself to focus; acutely aware of contraction intensity (especially without an epidural); doubts her ability to cope; apprehensive, restless, irritable; afraid of being alone — but may not want anyone to talk to or touch her

Note on cervical history: procedures and scarring on the cervix — e.g. a prior cervical LEEP for an abnormal Pap — can impede effacement and dilation and really extend out the latent phase.

Second Stage

Begins with complete cervical dilation and effacement, ends with the birth of the baby. She usually has an urge to push, and crowning occurs as the top of the head starts to come out of the introitus. Contractions are very strong and close together.

PatientNormal Length of Pushing
First-time momMay push up to 3 hours — that's considered normal
Previous birthsUsually less than an hour; the average is about 15–20 minutes

Emotionally she is often relieved that the acute pain is over and that she can finally push, and she has a sense of control because she is actively involved. She may also become frightened knowing it's coming soon, feel overwhelmed by the pressure and pain, and be very fatigued from a long labor.

The Mechanisms of Labor

How the baby actually moves and rotates in order to be born, in order:

MechanismWhat Happens
1. EngagementThe largest part of the fetal presenting part passes through the pelvic inlet
2. DescentAffected by pressure of the amniotic fluid, contractions, pushing effort, and extension of the fetus
3. FlexionThe head and chin are brought closer to the chest, so the smaller head diameter presents to the pelvic outlet
4. Internal rotationThe fetal head rotates from occiput transverse to occiput anterior
5. ExtensionThe occiput comes, then the face, then the chin delivers
6. External rotationThe head rotates back to the OT position; the provider delivers the anterior, then the posterior shoulder
7. ExpulsionBirth of the baby

Third Stage

The third stage should last no longer than 30 minutes. If it does, there is a risk for hemorrhage and retained placenta.

Signs of placental separation

  • A globular uterus
  • The uterus rises in the abdomen
  • A gush or trickle of blood
  • More and more of the umbilical cord becomes visible

Management & the two placental sides

  • Oxytocin is given as an IV bolus right after the placenta delivers, to decrease blood loss.
  • Do fundal massage to check that the uterus stays firm.
  • Dirty Duncan = the maternal side, which was attached to the uterus.
  • Shiny Schultze = the fetal membrane side (chorion and amnion).

IV oxytocin is used not only to induce labor but also to control postpartum bleeding.

Fourth Stage

From delivery of the placenta up to 4 hours postpartum. A great time to promote mother–infant bonding, and a time of physiologic readjustment.

Expected Blood Loss

Birth TypeBlood Loss Should Be
Vaginal deliveryLess than 500 mL (average estimated blood loss 250–500 mL)
Cesarean birthLess than 1,000 mL

Fourth-Stage Assessment & Findings

What You Assess / ObserveDetails
Fundus, perineum, vaginal bleedingEvery 15 minutes for the first hour
TemperatureAt the beginning and end of the recovery period
Post-anesthesia careWatch for returning sensation; check oxygen saturation
BladderCheck for fullness. The bladder is often hypotonic — she may need a bedpan or an in-and-out catheter.
UterusShould stay contracted and midline in the abdomen, between the symphysis pubis and the umbilicus
ShakingA CNS response a couple of hours after birth — the body was working hard with a lot of adrenaline pumping, and that strenuous work has now stopped. She also has fluid and heat loss; warm blankets help relax the muscles.
Thirst / hungerPatients are often very thirsty and hungry
Fundal assessment technique: one hand supports the lower uterine segment, while the other hand rests on top of the abdomen and presses down to feel the top of the fundus. This is done to help control vaginal bleeding and keep the uterus firm.
Kangaroo care (skin to skin) is encouraged right after birth: it helps regulate the baby's body temperature and heart rate, increases bonding, increases oxytocin release by the mother, and stimulates her breasts — especially good if she wants to breastfeed.

Self-Test Flashcards

What are the Five Ps of labor and birth?
Passage, Passenger, Position, Powers, and Psyche.
Which landmark of the pelvis marks zero station?
The ischial spines — the smallest, narrowest part of the pelvis.
Which of the four pelvis shapes is most favorable for a vaginal birth?
The gynecoid pelvis — the classic female shape, in about half of women.
What is the single biggest sign that tells true labor from false labor?
Cervical change — true labor produces progressive dilation and effacement.
What shape is the anterior fontanelle, and is it the larger or the smaller one?
Diamond-shaped, and it is the larger of the two fontanelles.
How dilated is the cervix during the transition phase of the first stage?
8–10 cm.
What does contraction frequency measure, and in what unit?
The time from the start of one contraction to the start of the next — measured in minutes.
What is the Ferguson reflex?
The maternal urge to bear down, from endogenous oxytocin released when vaginal stretch receptors are activated.
How long should the third stage of labor take, and what's the risk if it runs longer?
No longer than 30 minutes; beyond that there is a risk of hemorrhage and retained placenta.
What are the signs that the placenta has separated?
A globular uterus, the uterus rising in the abdomen, a gush or trickle of blood, and the umbilical cord lengthening.
What is the expected blood loss for a vaginal birth and for a cesarean birth?
Less than 500 mL for a vaginal birth; less than 1,000 mL for a cesarean.
What are the cardinal movements (mechanisms) of labor, in order?
Engagement, descent, flexion, internal rotation, extension, external rotation, and expulsion.
