Intrapartum Complications

Rupture of Membranes Problems

TermDefinition
PROMPremature rupture of membranes — the water breaks before the onset of labor
PPROMPreterm premature rupture of membranes — the water breaks before 37 weeks
ProlongedMembranes ruptured for greater than 18 hours

Possible Causes

  • Infection
  • Previous history of early rupture
  • Hydramnios
  • Multiple pregnancy
  • Urinary tract infection
  • Amniocentesis
  • Placenta previa
  • Placental abruption
  • Trauma
  • Incompetent cervix
  • History of a LEEP or laser conization of the cervix
  • Bleeding during pregnancy
  • Maternal genital tract anomalies

Fetal & newborn risks

  • Respiratory distress syndrome — fluid is needed for lung development, so early rupture means the lungs may be less developed
  • Fetal sepsis
  • Malpresentation — the baby needs fluid to rotate into an optimal position
  • Prolapse of the umbilical cord
  • Non-reassuring fetal heart rate pattern and cord compression (you may see variables — less cushion for the cord)
  • Premature birth — broken water can increase contractions
  • A planned delivery may be needed because of infection

Assessment & care

  • Determine how long the water has been broken and assess gestational age — term or preterm?
  • Watch for signs and symptoms of infection
  • Assess hydration status — poor maternal intake won't support her amniotic fluid volume
  • Assess fetal status with non-stress tests and biophysical profiles
  • Assess childbirth preparation and coping — an early rupture (e.g., 27 weeks) can mean hospitalization until birth, with no car seat or classes ready
  • Encourage rest, particularly on her left side; provide comfort measures and education about the plan of care

Corticosteroids may be considered to enhance fetal lung maturity when the baby is preterm (under 37 weeks).

Preterm Labor

Labor occurring between 20 and 36 6/7 weeks gestation.

Signs & Symptoms

  • Uterine contractions — about 8 in an hour, or 4 in 20 minutes
  • Cervical change or dilation
  • Mild menstrual-like cramps felt low in the abdomen
  • Constant or intermittent pelvic pressure
  • Possibly ruptured membranes
  • A low, dull backache — constant or intermittent
  • Increased vaginal discharge

Strongest Predictors of Preterm Birth

PredictorWhat It Means
Fetal fibronectin (fFN)Fibronectin is the protein that keeps the amniotic sac "glued" to the lining of the uterus. A positive result means the glue has been disrupted — increased risk of preterm labor or birth. You don't want fetal fibronectin in the cervical secretions.
Cervical lengthMeasured by ultrasound probe. Shortening or thinning — particularly less than 25 mm before term — is abnormal
History & infectionA previous preterm birth, or a current infection, are both risks

Tocolytics — Medications to Stop Preterm Contractions

MedicationDose / RouteNursing Considerations
Nifedipine 10–20 mg PO every 4 hours Monitor blood pressure closely — you are giving it to relax the uterine muscle, not because she is hypertensive. Do not give if the systolic is less than 90.
Magnesium sulfate IV 4–6 g bolus load over about 20 minutes, then 2–4 g/hour Monitor alertness, respirations, blood pressure, reflexes, and I&O closely. She may have some muscle weakness, a lowered blood pressure, and lowered reflexes — but reflexes should still be present.
Terbutaline Subcutaneous, for acute use only — about 2–3 days She may report a racing heart, flushing, and trembling (side effects). Hold rule covered again here — review in Intrapartum II
Progesterone Suppositories at nighttime Raises the progesterone level to help her stay pregnant longer
Magnesium safety, same as oxytocin: use an IV pump, hook it to the lowest port on the primary IV line (never as a secondary line), and put no more than 2 hours of fluid in the volume control.
Two extra benefits when preterm birth is expected: Corticosteroids — usually betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 12–24 hours apart (sometimes dexamethasone); the "steroid window" is the 48 hours after the first dose, and the steroid causes a release of surfactant to speed lung maturity. Magnesium sulfate is also neuroprotective — it may decrease the risk of intracranial hemorrhage and necrotizing enterocolitis, so a patient may be on magnesium for that reason as well as for contractions.

Cervical Insufficiency

Also called incompetent cervix — the painless dilation of the cervix without contractions. It is a cervical defect, with a shortened cervical length. It may be diagnosed after previous miscarriages that occurred without contractions.

Cervical length covered again here — review in Preterm Labor if needed

CareDetail
Serial cervical ultrasoundsTransvaginal ultrasounds beginning at 16–24 weeks to follow cervical length
Bed restIf indicated — but strict bed rest at home the whole time is not required
Progesterone supplementationOften prescribed
CerclageSurgical closure of the cervix using suture. Antibiotics may be needed. Also done prophylactically in triplets or quadruplets, and sometimes twins. Monitor for bleeding.
At deliveryThe suture may be cut and a vaginal birth permitted, or left in place with a C-section and removed later

Teach her to call the provider for: lower back pain, pelvic pressure, changes in vaginal discharge, uterine cramping, or bleeding after the cerclage. Having a cerclage does not mean she must deliver early, and she does not need antibiotics her entire pregnancy — hopefully the cervix stabilizes and she can be somewhat active.

Placenta Previa vs. Placental Abruption

The single biggest difference is abdominal pain and how hard the abdomen feels.

FeaturePlacenta PreviaPlacental Abruption
What it is The placenta implants in the lower uterine segment. As that segment contracts and dilates, the placental villi tear from the uterine wall Premature separation of a normally implanted placenta from the uterine wall
Bleeding Bright red, painless bleeding May be visible or concealed (with a central abruption); a complete abruption causes massive bleeding
Pain & uterus Uterine tenderness usually absent; the uterus is soft and relaxed Pain is usually severe; the uterus is very tender and feels firm or stone-hard; the abdomen may enlarge and change shape
Risk factors Many previous pregnancies, older maternal age, increasing gestational age, prior C-section, recent spontaneous or induced abortion, cigarette smoking, male fetus Maternal hypertension, domestic violence, abdominal trauma, fibroids, uterine over-distension, fetal growth restriction, male fetus, elevated AFP, smoking, alcohol, cocaine, short umbilical cord, multiple deliveries, advanced maternal age
If the placenta covers all or part of the cervix, do NOT perform a vaginal exam — you can puncture a hole into the placenta.

Types of Previa

TypePosition
CompleteThe placenta totally covers the cervix
PartialPartially covers the cervix
MarginalNear the cervix
Low-lyingNot on or touching the cervix, but fairly close

Previa care: assess for bleeding (a blood transfusion may be needed with active bleeding), assess vital signs and fetal status with fetal heart rate monitoring, watch the contraction monitor, and anticipate an unengaged presenting part — the head can't move down because the placenta is in the way — and possibly a transverse lie. Obtain consents for a C-section, and tocolytics such as magnesium sulfate or nifedipine may be given if she is bleeding or cramping.

Types and Grades of Abruption

TypeWhat Happens
MarginalThe placenta separates at its edges
CentralSeparates centrally — bleeding is often concealed; she may just have a very hard abdomen that keeps enlarging as blood accumulates
CompleteTotal separation with massive vaginal bleeding
GradeSeparation & Bleeding
Grade 1Mild separation with slight bleeding — about 48% of cases
Grade 2Partial abruption with moderate bleeding
Grade 3Complete separation with moderate to severe bleeding

Abruption care: monitor uterine activity and how the baby is tolerating it, monitor complaints of abdominal pain and abdominal size, and check for the development of DIC. Immediate priority is maintaining maternal cardiovascular status because of the blood loss, and a C-section is often the safest option. An abruption does not automatically mean an emergency delivery — it depends on the degree and how the baby is tolerating it.

Multiple Gestation

Increased risks

  • Preterm labor · uterine dysfunction · abnormal fetal presentations
  • Instrumental or C-section birth · postpartum hemorrhage
  • Higher mortality than a single fetus; decreased intrauterine growth rate
  • Increased fetal anomalies, cord accidents, and cerebral palsy

Common complaints & comfort

  • Shortness of breath or dyspnea on exertion — but not at rest
  • Backache and round ligament pain (the uterus is much larger)
  • Heartburn, pelvic or suprapubic pressure, pedal edema
  • Rest as much as possible, elevate legs and feet, side-lying
  • Back discomfort: pelvic rocking, good posture, good body mechanics

Education & management: nutrition for a multiple pregnancy — prenatal vitamins, increased folic acid, and greater total weight gain (perhaps 40–45 lb versus 25–35 lb for a singleton, depending on where she starts). Watch for fetal activity from all babies and know the signs of preterm labor. Ultrasounds check growth and fluid. These patients are seen much more often. At delivery: consult anesthesia, have blood products available, and monitor all babies (sometimes multiple monitors). The method of birth may change once labor begins depending on how the babies are presenting; many choose a scheduled cesarean.

Amniotic Fluid Problems

ConditionDefinitionConcerns
Hydramnios
polyhydramnios		 More than 2,000 mL of amniotic fluid Increases the risk of preterm labor. Some fluid may need to be removed by amniocentesis
Oligohydramnios Less than 500 mL Monitor the baby closely — renal and urinary malformations are risk factors; skin and skeletal abnormalities; underdeveloped lungs; and a risk of cord compression with less fluid
Amniotic fluid embolism. Amniotic fluid leaks into the maternal circulation — through a small tear in the amnion or chorion, through the uterus during placental separation, or through cervical tears under pressure from the contracting uterus — and an embolism blocks the vessels of the lungs. It is very rare but has an 80–90% maternal mortality rate.

Warning signs: chest pain, dyspnea, cyanosis, frothy sputum, tachycardia, hypotension, and possible massive hemorrhage.

Care: stabilize her cardiovascular and respiratory systems, infuse whole blood, and anticipate a central line to monitor for fluid overload. If she has not delivered, she needs an immediate delivery. During CPR on a woman 20 weeks or more pregnant, someone must push the uterus to the side — otherwise lying flat prevents good blood return.

Dysfunctional Labor Patterns

PatternDefinitionNursing Care
Hypertonic
tachysystole		 Contractions come too close together — a new one starts as soon as the last ends, so there is not enough time for rest and perfusion to the uterus and baby. Tachysystole covered again here — review in Intrapartum II Assess contractions, how the baby is tolerating it, and maternal vital signs. Provide comfort and support — she is in a lot of pain; help her change positions, offer back rubs. If oxytocin is running, turn it off. Terbutaline subcutaneously may be needed to relax and space out contractions. She may need sedation and pain medication.
Hypotonic Fewer than 2–3 contractions in 10 minutes, very low intensity — not effective at making cervical change Assess contractions, fetal tolerance, and vital signs. Consider CPD and rule out a malpresentation — the body's signal that this baby may not come out vaginally. Maintain adequate hydration and monitor for infection. She may need oxytocin to stimulate contractions and augment labor.

Post-Term Pregnancy

TermDefinition
PostdatesThe pregnancy has gone beyond the estimated due date
Post-termThe pregnancy has gone beyond 42 completed weeks

There is no good evidence that waiting past 42 weeks improves outcomes — the risk of complications greatly increases. Monitor with daily fetal movement counts, non-stress tests, and biophysical profiles, and discuss labor induction.

Maternal impacts

  • Perineal damage — the baby keeps growing
  • Increased risk of hemorrhage
  • Increased risk of C-section birth
  • Anxiety, worry, emotional fatigue; worry about being induced
  • Persistent, ongoing pregnancy discomforts

Fetal risks

  • Decreased placental perfusion — the placenta was not designed to last forever and shows signs of aging
  • Risk of oligohydramnios
  • Macrosomia — or the opposite, small for gestational age
  • Increased meconium-stained fluid, raising the risk of meconium aspiration syndrome
  • Malposition or malpresentation

Malposition, Malpresentation & Version

ProblemWhat It IsWhat Helps
Malposition For example a persistent occiput posterior position — the occiput is toward the mother's back, so the baby wants to come out face up. Anatomically harder to push out and move through the pelvis Rotate her side to side to encourage internal rotation; try knee-chest or hands and knees. The provider may need to manually rotate the fetal head during labor
Malpresentation A shoulder, brow, or face presentation, or a breech. Presentation types covered again here — review in Intrapartum I May require a version or a cesarean
External cephalic version (ECV) External manipulation of the maternal abdomen to turn the baby from a breech to a cephalic position See the care below
Internal podalic version An internal version, sometimes used to help deliver a second twin A much less common type of version

Nursing Care for an External Cephalic Version

  • Consent — she must know a C-section is a potential risk (the baby may not tolerate the manipulation, the placenta could tear, her water could break)
  • An IV in place
  • Ultrasound to see the baby's position, and repeated to check the heartbeat and position
  • Subcutaneous terbutaline if she is contracting
  • Fasting for 8 hours beforehand, because of the C-section risk
  • Monitor the baby before, during, and after, with a reactive NST
  • If she is Rh negative, give a prophylactic dose of RhoGAM — the manipulation can mix fetal and maternal blood

Non-Reassuring Fetal Status

Findings That Are Non-Reassuring

  • A variation of the heart rate from normal — bradycardia or tachycardia
  • Decreased fetal movement
  • Meconium-stained fluid
  • Persistent late decelerations or persistent severe variables

The goal is to figure out what is causing the problem in order to fix it — worked through with the UNCOIL mnemonic.

Umbilical Cord Prolapse

The umbilical cord precedes the presenting part and is compressed against the maternal pelvis.

Prevention first: if the water is broken and the presenting part is still high in the pelvis, the patient should stay in bed until the head settles against the cervix.

If the cord is felt during a vaginal exam:
1. Keep your gloved fingers in the vagina to relieve pressure and push up on the baby's head, off the cord.
2. Immediately drop the head of the bed into Trendelenburg to take the fetal head's pressure off the cord.
3. Notify the provider that there is a prolapsed cord.
4. Give oxygen as needed.
5. Prepare for a cesarean birth.

CPD, Macrosomia & Shoulder Dystocia

ConditionMaternal RisksFetal Risks
Cephalopelvic disproportion (CPD) Prolonged labor, increased risk of uterine rupture, increased chance of a forceps or vacuum birth, and most likely a C-section. True CPD should be delivered by cesarean. Increased risk of cord prolapse (the baby isn't moving down well), excessive molding of the head, bruising, and nerve trauma from trying to fit
Macrosomia
over 4,000 g		 Dysfunctional labor, uterine rupture, perineal lacerations, postpartum hemorrhage (the uterus is more distended), and a risk of shoulder dystocia Identify it before labor if possible: evaluate whether the pelvis is large enough, and use ultrasound for an estimated fetal weight. For a diabetic patient, keeping glucose in range decreases the chance of macrosomia. In labor, a lack of fetal descent should raise suspicion the baby is too large
Shoulder dystocia — the head delivers but the shoulders are trapped behind the pubic bone. Dangers are brain damage from hypoxia, brachial plexus damage, and umbilical cord occlusion.

What to do: lower the head of the bed, perform the McRoberts maneuver (rotate the legs up and out to widen the pelvis as much as possible), and the nurse applies suprapubic pressure with downward pressure on the pubic bone. Document the interventions and the length of time the dystocia lasted.

NEVER apply fundal pressure — it is contraindicated and can cause fetal and uterine harm.

Third & Fourth Stage Complications

ComplicationWhat to Know
Retained placenta Bleeding can be excessive and it may require manual removal by the provider; she may need a blood transfusion and is at increased risk for infection. Third-stage time limit covered again here — review in Intrapartum I
Subinvolution The uterus does not return to its pre-pregnancy size, and she is at increased risk for bleeding
Lacerations Spontaneous tearing, usually of the perineal area. Suspect a laceration when you see bright red bleeding despite a well-contracted uterus — the fundus feels firm, but bright red blood spurts when you put pressure on the uterus. Graded first through fourth degree. Assist with the repair and observe for bleeding and approximation (does the skin line up?)

Abnormal Placental Adherence

TypeHow Deep It Goes
NormalThe placenta attaches to the endometrium
AccretaThe chorionic villi attach through the endometrium, directly to the top of the myometrium
IncretaThe placenta invades into the myometrium (think: increta = invades)
PercretaThe myometrium is penetrated — it goes through and can attach to nearby organs such as the bowel or bladder (think: percreta = perforates)

Risk factors: placenta previa, previous uterine surgery, endometrial defects, maternal age and parity, increased C-section births, smoking, previous D&Cs, advanced maternal age, and IVF procedures. Management: monitor closely for bleeding before delivery and often deliver a little early; type and cross because of the high chance of transfusion. About two-thirds of women have an abdominal hysterectomy to prevent maternal hemorrhage, since the placenta will not detach, and a general surgeon may be needed for organ repair.

Perinatal Loss

Families move through stages of grief — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance.

Supporting the family

  • Help prepare them for the birth and the death of their baby
  • Involve counselors, chaplains, and perinatal bereavement teams
  • Support decision-making — including whether she goes to the postpartum floor or a different unit
  • Support viewing and holding the infant to help actualize the loss
  • Offer pictures, a memory box, and mementos; discuss baptism and burial options
  • Support siblings and other family members
  • She still needs postpartum care and good discharge planning, with referrals to community services
  • In Kentucky, notify KODA for organ donation any time there is a loss, even though stillborn infants are often not eligible
  • Say: "I'm so sorry for your loss. How can I help?" Listen, let them tell their story, and acknowledge that this baby was special and loved

What NOT to say

  • "At least you know you can get pregnant."
  • "You can always try to have another."
  • "These things happen for a reason." — losing a baby is never a good reason
  • "Be thankful for the kids you have." — true, but it doesn't take away the sting of this loss
  • "I know exactly how you feel." — no one knows how someone else feels, and it makes it about you
