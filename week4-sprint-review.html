Sprint Review Flashcards

What are the 5 components of the Bishop score?
Cervical dilation, cervical effacement, cervical position, cervical consistency (4 cervical) + fetal station (1 fetal). Know the components, not the point values.
What are some uses for an amniotomy (AROM)?
To induce labor, to augment labor, and to allow placement of internal monitors (FSE / IUPC — membranes must be ruptured).
Mechanical induction with a Foley bulb causes what to be released?
Prostaglandins — the bulb mimics the pressure of the baby's head against the cervix.
Which prostaglandin can be removed by pulling the connected string?
Dinoprostone (Cervidil) — a flat vaginal insert with a string.
After inserting misoprostol (Cytotec), how many hours must you wait before starting oxytocin?
At least 4 hours.
Does overuse of oxytocin increase the risk of postpartum hemorrhage?
Yes — the oxytocin receptors get saturated, so after delivery the uterus is less likely to clamp down.
Labor augmentation helps which type of uterine contractions?
Hypotonic contractions (too infrequent or too weak to make cervical change).
Why is a wedge placed under the RIGHT hip during a cesarean section?
To tilt her to the left and prevent supine hypotension (keeps the uterus off the vena cava). It is removed after the baby is born.
What anesthesia may be offered to a woman who does not want an epidural but has sharp, localized second-stage pain?
A pudendal block or local (perineal) anesthesia.
True or false: systemic pain medications cross the placental barrier.
True — so they reach the baby, whose liver and kidneys can't metabolize them well.
A multipara presents at 8 cm and wants anesthesia. What might be ordered?
A spinal or a combined spinal-epidural, nitrous oxide, or local anesthesia — something with a faster onset than a plain epidural, since she may deliver soon.
Which type of anesthesia poses the highest risk for maternal aspiration?
General anesthesia.
What are some indications for a cesarean birth?
Complete previa, CPD, abruption, active genital herpes, cord prolapse, failure to progress, tumors obstructing the birth canal, major congenital anomalies, and non-reassuring FHR.
What are some fetal/newborn risks of instrument (vacuum or forceps) deliveries?
Facial ecchymosis or edema, caput or cephalohematoma, transient facial paralysis, brachial plexus injury, cerebral hemorrhage, clavicle fracture, and elevated neonatal bilirubin.
Why is food limited while a woman is laboring?
To prevent maternal aspiration (pregnancy slows gastric emptying — she's treated as a 'full stomach').
Describe nursing care BEFORE an epidural/spinal.
Assess maternal status (especially BP) and fetal status and labor progress; start an IV and give a warmed LR bolus of 1000–1500 mL; help her into position (sitting on the side of the bed, back curved outward).
Describe nursing care DURING an epidural/spinal.
Continue assessing maternal (BP) and fetal status; help her hold the position; assist her breathing through contractions.
Describe nursing care AFTER an epidural/spinal.
Monitor BP, respirations, and FHR; watch for hypotension and correct it (extra fluid bolus, ephedrine 5–10 mg IV, oxygen 10 L by non-rebreather); give antiemetics as needed; catheterize if she can't void.
What four things must be completed before starting an induction of labor?
A reactive NST, a sterile vaginal exam with a Bishop score, informed consent, and vital signs.
What is the FIRST action after a woman reports her water leaking or breaking?
Assess the fetal heart rate — lost fluid means the cord can be compressed.
What are the advantages of cervical ripening?
A shorter labor and cervical effacement. (It does NOT lower PPH risk — it raises the risk of PPH and uterine rupture.)
What is the purpose of stripping (sweeping) the membranes in the office?
To separate the amniotic membrane from the lower uterine segment, releasing prostaglandins that stimulate contractions (not oxytocin, and without tearing the sac).
What interventions support labor augmentation?
Stripping membranes, amniotomy, oxytocin, and ambulation. (Pudendal anesthesia does not — it relieves pain.)
During an amnioinfusion, you see a moderate amount of clear fluid on the pad every hour. What do you do?
Document it as a normal finding — fluid in should equal fluid out. Stop the infusion only if no fluid returns and the resting tone rises.
How do you titrate oxytocin, and what must be in place first?
A reactive NST and a Bishop score first, with continuous fetal monitoring; increase 1–2 milliunits/min every 30 minutes. Never bolus it.
Name three risks of oxytocin.
Tachysystole (≥6 contractions/10 min), uterine rupture, water intoxication (acute hyponatremia), and postpartum hemorrhage after prolonged use.
A forceps delivery: when does the provider apply traction?
Only while the client pushes — never pull when she is not pushing. Document when the forceps go on and come off.
Does the skin incision always match the uterine incision in a cesarean?
No. Document both — the uterine incision is the one that matters (low transverse is safest; classical/vertical → future cesareans).
What disqualifies a patient from a VBAC?
A previous classical (vertical) uterine incision — also a prior myomectomy. She needs an adequate pelvis and in-house anesthesia.
Why give a pre-anesthetic fluid bolus before a spinal?
To prevent maternal hypotension (spinal/epidural anesthesia reliably drops the blood pressure).
What are contraindications to systemic analgesia in labor?
Imminent delivery / ready to push (newborn respiratory depression) and a Category III tracing.
What FHR change is expected after IV meperidine (Demerol)?
Decreased variability.
Systolic BP drops to 95 after anesthesia with a late decel — what do you do?
Oxygen by non-rebreather, turn her to the side, give ephedrine, and start an IV fluid bolus. Do NOT raise the head of the bed.
Is precipitous (fast) labor a complication of an epidural?
No — epidurals can slow labor slightly. Hypotension, urinary retention, and itching (pruritus) are the complications.
In what two situations is oxytocin NOT acceptable to give?
With significant variable decelerations or with late decelerations (it would further stress the baby).
When do you immediately discontinue oxytocin?
When you see fetal tachycardia with absent variability (a sign of fetal acidosis) — she needs delivery, not more oxytocin.
What complication do you assess for after a forceps-assisted delivery?
Vaginal (and cervical) lacerations.
Best prenatal teaching to reduce anxiety about labor pain?
Explore relaxation techniques and encourage prenatal/childbirth education.
A client with mitral stenosis is in active labor — how do you promote cardiac function?
Side-lying with head and shoulders elevated; avoid the Valsalva maneuver.
Signs of intravascular injection during an epidural/spinal test dose?
Ringing in the ears, a funny metallic taste, or difficulty breathing → stop the anesthesia (the medication reached a vessel).
What is a wet tap, and how is it treated?
A CSF leak from the puncture site causing a positional headache (worse upright). Treat with rest, fluids, pain meds, and time; if needed, a blood patch seals the leak.
Why is betamethasone given for PPROM?
To enhance fetal lung maturity (surfactant release). Given under 34 weeks, 2 doses; try not to deliver within 48 hours of the first.
What is the priority concern with prolonged rupture of membranes (>18 hr)?
Infection.
Is 'no cervical change or dilation' a sign of preterm labor?
No — cervical change and dilation ARE signs of labor. Preterm-labor signs include cramps, ruptured membranes, and a low dull backache.
Terbutaline was given and the patient reports her heart racing and feeling jittery — first action?
Reassure her that this is an expected side effect (you'd have checked her HR first and held it if ≥120).
Best way to confirm a suspected placenta previa?
An ultrasound — NOT a vaginal exam, which could puncture the placenta.
Greatest risk for a marginal previa 15 minutes after a cesarean?
Hemorrhage (the lower uterine segment doesn't contract well).
What single symptom differentiates abruption from previa?
Abdominal pain — abruption is painful with a rigid, board-like uterus; previa is painless bleeding with a soft uterus.
What is a normal amniotic fluid index (AFI)?
5–25 cm. (An AFI of 15 is normal.)
Which is NOT a fetal risk of oligohydramnios?
Pulmonary HYPERplasia — it's actually pulmonary HYPOplasia. Real risks: renal/urinary malformations, skin/skeletal abnormalities, and cord compression.
Symptoms of an amniotic fluid embolism?
Chest pain, frothy sputum, and dyspnea, with hypotension (not hypertension). Support the cardiovascular and respiratory systems; displace the uterus during CPR.
What factor is associated with dysfunctional labor?
An abnormal fetal presentation (e.g., transverse) — not maternal age.
Which intervention is WRONG for uterine tachysystole?
Increasing the oxytocin. Do everything else — assess, comfort, reposition, pain meds — and turn the oxytocin down or off.
Risks of a post-term (≥42 week) pregnancy?
Decreased fetal perfusion, SGA, maternal perineal damage, and emotional fatigue (plus oligohydramnios and increased meconium-stained fluid).
First nursing action for a prolapsed umbilical cord?
Keep gloved fingers in the vagina to lift the presenting part off the cord (then Trendelenburg, oxygen, notify, prep for cesarean).
After the head delivers, there is shoulder dystocia — what does the nurse anticipate?
McRoberts: flex the hips and bring both knees toward the chest, plus suprapubic pressure. Never fundal pressure.
Give the order of definitions: accreta, increta, percreta.
Accreta Attaches (villi attach to the myometrium), increta Invades (into the myometrium), percreta Perforates (through, possibly to bladder/bowel).
Discharge teaching after a cerclage for cervical insufficiency?
Report any uterine cramping or low back pain. She does NOT need strict bed rest, a cesarean, or lifelong antibiotics.
