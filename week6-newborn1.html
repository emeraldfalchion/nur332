Apgar Score

The Apgar measures how well the baby is transitioning to extrauterine life. Done at 1 minute and 5 minutes of life. Each of the 5 categories scores 0, 1, or 2. Memorize this.

APGAR: Appearance (color) · Pulse (heart rate) · Grimace (reflex irritability) · Activity (muscle tone) · Respirations (respiratory effort).

Each category scores 0, 1, or 2. Using heart rate as the example: absent = 0, below 100 = 1, 100 or more = 2 — the same 0/1/2 pattern applies to each of the five signs.

Initiating Respirations

Four types of change trigger the newborn's first breaths. At term about 80–100 mL of fluid remains in the lungs; production drops in the 2–4 days before birth.

ChangeHow It Triggers Breathing
ChemicalThe first breath/gasp is triggered by an increase in PCO₂ and a decrease in pH and PO₂, which stimulates the brain's respiratory center
MechanicalDuring birth the fetal chest is compressed and fluid is squeezed out. This is why C-section babies (and very quick/precipitous deliveries) are more prone to retained lung fluid — they miss that squeeze
ThermalThe sudden drop in environmental temperature after birth stimulates the baby to cry and breathe. Avoid prolonged cold exposure/cold stress by keeping the baby warm
SensoryLight, sound, gravity, and touch also stimulate respirations
Breathing patterns: the first 2 hours the rate may run 60–70, then settle to 30–60. Periodic breathing — pauses up to 20 seconds — is normal (another reason to count for a full minute). Apnea = pauses greater than 20 seconds and is abnormal.
Signs of respiratory distress: nasal flaring, intercostal or xiphoid retractions, expiratory grunting or sighing, seesaw respirations, and tachypnea.

Respiratory Complications

Transient Tachypnea of the Newborn (TTN)

Progressive respiratory distress noted from at least 6 hours of age until about 3 days; should clear by ~72 hours. A chest X-ray shows hyper-expanded lungs. Risk factors: large for gestational age, late preterm, maternal over-sedation or bleeding, prolapsed cord, breech birth, maternal diabetes, and cesarean birth.

Do NOT orally feed an infant with a respiratory rate greater than 60 — aspiration risk. Support with an oxy-hood or cannula, and IV fluids or tube feedings as needed.

Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)

The alveoli (terminal air sacs where gas exchange happens) are the last structures to form. The younger the baby, the fewer the alveoli and the more severe the RDS. Surfactant production is stimulated by giving the mother a corticosteroid (betamethasone) and by rupturing membranes.

Betamethasone dosing & the steroid window covered again here — review in Intrapartum Complications if needed

Treatment of RDS

  • Oxygen therapy — CPAP, mechanical ventilation, or high-frequency ventilation
  • Surfactant replacement
  • Thermoregulation via an incubator (avoid cold stress and extra work of breathing)
  • ECMO (a modified heart-lung machine) for severely ill babies, letting the lungs rest/heal

Circulatory Transition

Assess the apical heart rate for a full minute at rest (110–160; drops in sleep, rises with crying). Check the brachial and femoral pulses bilaterally, and capillary refill < 2–3 seconds. Blood pressure is highest right after birth and lowest around 3 hours of age.

Newborn Circulatory ChangeWhat Happens
1. Vascular resistance shiftIncreased systemic vascular resistance and decreased pulmonary vascular resistance — enhances perfusion of the body systems
2. Foramen ovale closesWithin 1–2 hours of birth (the opening between the atria)
3. Ductus arteriosus closesBlood now goes from the aorta to the pulmonary artery
4. Ductus venosus closesForces perfusion of the liver

Thermoregulation

The goal is to keep the core temperature within a narrow range. Thermoregulation is tied to the rate of metabolism and oxygen consumption; flexion decreases surface area and helps prevent heat loss.

Mechanism of Heat LossDefinition
ConvectionLoss of heat from the warm body surface to cooler air
RadiationHeat transferred to a cooler surface/object not in direct contact with the baby
EvaporationLoss of heat when water turns to vapor (e.g., a wet baby)
ConductionLoss of heat to a cooler surface by direct contact
Why babies lose heat easily: larger surface-area-to-mass ratio, decreased subcutaneous fat, increased body water, immature skin (evaporative loss), poorly developed metabolic responses (babies can't shiver), and altered skin blood flow. Preterm babies are at even higher risk — they lack a flexed posture and have thin skin with capillaries near the surface.

Cold Stress vs. Hyperthermia

Cold Stress

  • Acrocyanosis; cool, mottled, or pale skin
  • Hypoglycemia, transient hyperglycemia
  • Bradycardia, tachypnea, restless/shallow/irregular respirations
  • Signs of respiratory distress, apnea, hypoxemia, metabolic acidosis
  • If uncorrected: lethargic, poor tone, feeble cry, poor feeding, weight loss

Hyperthermia

  • Causes: incubator/radiant warmer/warm room, maternal fever or epidural, phototherapy/sunlight, excessive bundling, infection, CNS disorder, dehydration
  • Signs: tachycardia, tachypnea or apnea, warm/flushed extremities, perspiration (term), lethargy, poor tone, poor feeding, irritable with a weak cry
  • Care: move away from the heat source, undress partially/fully, lower incubator air temp, and breastfeed frequently to replace fluids

Non-shivering thermogenesis: since babies can't shiver, skin receptors sensing cold trigger the use of brown fat for heat. Brown fat forms around 26–28 weeks gestation and continues until 3–5 weeks after birth; it's found in the mid-scapular area, around the neck, axilla, trachea/esophagus, abdominal aorta, kidneys, and adrenal glands. A radiant warmer (or kangaroo care against mom's skin) helps stabilize temperature.

Measurements

Averages for familiarity — not memorization. Average term weight ~7 lb 8 oz; term babies may lose up to 5–10% of birth weight (preterm up to 15%). Infants double birth weight by ~6 months and triple it by 12 months. Average length 18–22 inches; the head circumference is greater than the chest circumference at birth. Babies are weighed naked (account for the chucks pad); length is measured heel to head.

Head, Eyes, Ears & Mouth

Head

The head is proportionately large (~¼ of the body). Six cranial bones connect at sutures and fontanelles. Fontanelles should be soft and flat: sunken → dehydration; bulging → vigorous crying or increased intracranial pressure. Sutures may be approximated, separated, or overriding — overriding reflects molding (bones shifting to fit the pelvis), which settles into a normal shape with time.

Self-Test Flashcards

What do the 5 letters of APGAR stand for?
Appearance (color), Pulse (heart rate), Grimace (reflex irritability), Activity (muscle tone), Respirations. Scored 0–2 each, at 1 and 5 minutes.
Apgar heart rate: what score for absent, below 100, and 100 or more?
Absent = 0, below 100 = 1, 100 or more = 2.
What are the normal newborn pulse and respiratory rates, and how long do you count each?
Pulse 110–160 (apical, full minute); respirations 30–60 (full minute).
How long a breathing pause is normal (periodic breathing) vs. abnormal (apnea)?
Pauses up to 20 seconds = normal periodic breathing; greater than 20 seconds = apnea (abnormal).
Why are cesarean-born babies more prone to retained lung fluid?
They miss the mechanical chest compression of a vaginal birth that squeezes fluid out of the lungs.
You should not orally feed a newborn if the respiratory rate is above what?
Above 60 — aspiration risk.
Name the four mechanisms of newborn heat loss.
Convection (to cooler air), radiation (to a cooler object not touching), evaporation (water to vapor), conduction (to a cooler surface by contact).
How do babies make heat if they can't shiver?
Non-shivering thermogenesis using brown fat (forms 26–28 weeks, lasts to 3–5 weeks after birth).
Acrocyanosis vs. central cyanosis — which is normal?
Acrocyanosis (blue hands and feet) is normal in the first 24 hours. Central/circumoral cyanosis is abnormal — the baby should always be centrally pink.
Caput succedaneum vs. cephalohematoma — which crosses suture lines?
Caput crosses the suture lines; a cephalohematoma does NOT (and carries an increased jaundice risk).
A sunken fontanelle vs. a bulging fontanelle — what does each suggest?
Sunken = dehydration; bulging = vigorous crying or increased intracranial pressure.
Why should a baby with hypospadias not be circumcised?
He needs to see a pediatric urologist first — the foreskin tissue may be needed for repair.
